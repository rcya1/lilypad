Nonparametric Tests

So far we made an assumption about a distribution and then tested what the parameters of that distribution could be

Instead, non-parametric tests are tests for the entire distribution

  • i.e. whether XXX follows a normal distribution or not

      • By the CLT, this tells us that F^\hat{F}F^ converges to FFF asymptotically

Kolmogorov Lilliefors Test

  • Suppose we wanted to specifically test whether FFF is Gaussian or not
  • We could create a cdf F0F_0F0​ that is Gaussian and matches the mean and variance of the sample
  • This would be ok, but TTT in this case would not actually have a KS distribution because we matched up the first two moments
  • Instead, it follows a KL distribution and we could use those instead
    • Note: the KL rejection intervals are always smaller than the KS
    • So this means that the KS will retain the null in more cases than KL
    • So using the KS is ok but will give worse results

Permutation Test

  • This is a 2-sample test where we have a set of samples XXX and YYY and we want to test if FX=FYF_X = F_YFX​=FY​ or not
    • I.e. test a simulation of the world with and without the Higgs boson and use that to determine if the Higgs boson exists or not
  • If we computed the statistic T=sup⁡t∣F^X(t)−F^Y(t)∣T = \sup_t \abs{\hat{F}_X(t) - \hat{F}_Y(t)}T=supt​​F^X​(t)−F^Y​(t)​:
    • This would actually depend on the underlying distribution FFF and not be possible to calculate in most cases
  • If we computed the statistic ∣X‾−Y‾∣\abs{\overline{X} - \overline{Y}}​X−Y​
    • If the means of the distribution are the same, but are fundamentally different, then we would be committing a large type II error
    • We also still don't know FFF
  • The permutation test is based on the second idea and resolves the second issue, but not the first issue
    • We will actually compute ∣X‾−Y‾∣\abs{\overline{X} - \overline{Y}}​X−Y​
    • We will then attempt to compute the quantiles of this
    • This still fails if they happen to have the same mean
  • Under the null hypothesis, all of the data is the same and it shouldn't matter if we shuffle them around
  • Under the null hypothesis, we were equally likely to have seen any of the arrangements of the data
  • There are a total of 2n!2n!2n! ways to rearrange the data, and then we could just split the samples into left and right halves and take the difference in sample means
  • Therefore, if we just compute all possible permutations, we have 2n!2n!2n! samples for what the difference between left and right halves could be
    • We could compute the α\alphaα th percentile for this to get the rejection region
    • In reality, we can't compute 2n!2n !2n! permutations, so we just do a random sample
